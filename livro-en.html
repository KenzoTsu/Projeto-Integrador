The Man Who Counted

In the book "The Man Who Calculated", written by Malba Tahan and first published in 1938, the work combines mathematics, adventure, and philosophy, addressing numerical concepts in a creative and accessible way. The plot is set in the Middle East and tells the stories and adventures of Beremiz Samir, a young Persian endowed with incredible mathematical skills.

The plot

The plot follows Beremiz and his companion, Hank-Tade-Mai, as they travel to Baghdad. Along the way, the protagonist uses his numerical skills to resolve disputes, decipher riddles, and gain the trust of powerful rulers. Each chapter presents challenges that Beremiz overcomes with intelligence, demonstrating how mathematics can be a practical and transformative tool in people's lives.

Development

Upon arriving in Baghdad, Beremiz quickly draws the attention of powerful figures, including the caliph and his advisors. He is invited to solve complex problems and respond to intellectual challenges that test his mathematical and philosophical skills. In one of the passages, he faces a group of court mathematicians, solving a series of problems ranging from simple arithmetic to questions of logic and geometry.

Denouement

In addition to his mathematical adventures, Beremiz also experiences moments of introspection and philosophy. He is often confronted with moral and ethical questions, and his wisdom goes beyond numbers. He is a serene figure who values justice, harmony, and the application of knowledge for the common good.
